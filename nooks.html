What Nooks is doing is deceptively hard. Coaching a sales rep during a live call sounds simple until you sit with the latency budget. Transcription, inference, and context retrieval all have to run in real time, over a voice channel, while a human conversation is happening. The margin for error is essentially zero. A suggestion that arrives two seconds late is worse than no suggestion at all.

Having worked on real-time communications infrastructure, I think about this problem a lot. The stack required to make AI feel instantaneous over voice is a different animal from the one that makes it feel fast in a chat interface. Buffering, jitter, and codec variance all compound, and none of it is forgiving. When a product like Nooks is smooth enough that sales teams keep it running during live calls, that smoothness is the tell. It means the hard parts underneath were taken seriously.

The Market

A nine-billion-dollar category, actively consolidating.

The sales engagement market sits at roughly nine billion dollars in 2026, growing at sixteen percent annually. Underneath that healthy-looking number, something more interesting is happening. Gartner formalized a new category in 2025 called Revenue Action Orchestration, which collapses sales engagement, conversation intelligence, and revenue forecasting into a single platform. The Clari-Salesloft merger is the clearest proof: a revenue intelligence company and a sales engagement company combining to cover the full workflow. The standalone tools are getting absorbed.

The layer getting absorbed first is basic sequencing. Automated email queues, call scheduling, follow-up cadences. This is exactly the kind of workflow AI agents replace. Analysts project that forty percent of enterprise software will embed AI agents by end of 2026, up from under five percent in 2025. The commodity layer of outbound sales is going first. What remains is the question.

Gong is strong on post-call analysis but lives entirely in the past tense. Orum scales high-volume dialing but lacks coaching depth and has stagnated after layoffs in 2023. Salesloft, now merging with Clari, is broadening toward the orchestration model but real-time in-call intelligence is not its core. Apollo is a data and prospecting powerhouse with limited presence in the live conversation.

The Engineering

A budget measured in milliseconds.

Chat assistants get to think in seconds. A live call does not. Everything between the moment a prospect stops talking and the moment a rep needs to respond has to fit inside a window you can feel, and every stage of the pipeline spends from the same small account.

The fact that Nooks has made this smooth enough for live use says something real about the infrastructure beneath it. The forty-three-million-dollar Series B and the Seattle engineering push, zero to six engineers in short order with an office opening, are consistent with a team that knows the hardest problems are still ahead.

The stack that makes AI feel instantaneous over voice is a different problem from the one that makes it feel fast in chat.

The Frontier

What does not commoditize.

The consolidation wave in sales tech is eating the commodity layer first. Basic sequencing, automated emails, call queues, follow-up cadences. These are exactly the kind of workflows AI agents absorb. The Clari-Salesloft merger is a bet that the future belongs to full-stack orchestration platforms, not point solutions. That pressure will force smaller players to either find defensible depth or get acquired.

What an AI agent cannot replace is the conversation itself. Specifically, the eight seconds after a prospect raises a hard objection, when a well-timed response reframes the deal and a fumbled one loses it. Post-call analysis tells you what happened, which is useful but late. Parallel dialing gets you more at-bats, which is useful but shallow. Neither is present when the conversation turns. That is the layer Nooks is building toward, and it is the one that requires sub-second latency engineering most sales-tech companies have not had to solve.

The question I keep sitting with Does real-time in-call AI stay a coaching signal for a human rep, or does it eventually become a more active co-pilot? And as AI agents take over the prospecting and sequencing layer, what does the SDR role actually become? Nooks seems to be building toward an answer. The Seattle investment reads less like an office opening and more like a signal that the technically hard problems are still ahead of them.

That is the bet worth watching. Not whether the market grows (it will) but whether the real-time voice layer turns out to be a durable moat or a feature that gets bundled into something broader. From where I sit, the engineering required to do it well argues for the former.
